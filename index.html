Zach Dicken - Game Programmer

For the ‘Continue?’ jam, I worked with a team of two to create a game in 48 hours. As a programmer, I was unfamiliar with the engine (Godot 3.6) at the time, forcing me to learn from the other programmer as I worked. This was also my first jam and I wasn’t really used to short-form game development. Many learned techniques from other engines no longer applied in the same way, so being able to research efficiently was crucial. I programmed the game’s interactables, including the enemies and powerups, which required coordination with the other programmer to establish interaction between them. Having to learn Godot’s signal system under a time constraint was difficult, but the experience helped me in future jams significantly. After the jam concluded, our game was posted on itch.io and reviewed by game review streams, as well as being used as data for the jam’s organizer.

Alongside another programmer and two artists, I programmed a multiplayer party game in 72 hours for the Global Game Jam 2024. As I was proficient with the engine at the time (Godot 4.2), the main challenge was creating a concept that fit the jam’s odd theme. We went through a lot of iterations of our idea and had to do plenty of brainstorming as we worked. Additionally, the multiplayer aspect was a mechanic none of us had ever dived into before. This meant a lot of research on the programming side. GitHub was also a valuable tool when working in a tight schedule, as sharing assets and code between machines was integral. In the end, we presented the game at our GGJ Jam Space to our fellow jammers, talking extensively about our design process and challenges we faced. Our team gave and received feedback on the games presented at the jam.

itch.io


For this project, I programmed in C# in Unity to create a finished game in six weeks. My team consisted of two 3D artists and two programmers. We held weekly meetings to collaborate and set goals to complete by our next meeting. The largest challenge we were forced to overcome was the alternate controller, which consisted of a record that was spun in order to interact with the game. Therefore, we were essentially limited to only two inputs and had to base our ideas and programming around it. The game saw plenty of different iterations, but generally improved steadily each week. Our game was presented at the Music and Gaming Festival (MAGFest) in a collection with other alternate control games. We appeared as a team in order to represent our game at the festival.
